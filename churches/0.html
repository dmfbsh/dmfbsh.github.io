The History of the Church of England (with a bias towards Shropshire)

The history of Christianity started nearly 2000 years ago. This document outlines the major events that have shaped the Church of England to be as we know it today. This includes:

  • The Ministry of Jesus and the birth of Christianity
  • The spread of Christianity in the Roman Empire
  • The early arrival of Christianity in Britain
  • The mission sent by Pope Gregory to convert Britain
  • The conversion of the kingdom of Mercia
  • Monasticism and church building in post-conquest Britain
  • The crusades and conflict between the English kings and the pope
  • The English Reformation and the Dissolution of the Monasteries
  • The impact of the English Civil War and the Puritans
  • The church restoration and building in the Victorian era

Shropshire has played a part in this history; events of relevance to the county are highlighted in the following notes.

St Bartholomew, Tong

Shropshire has a largely rural church heritage with over 300 Anglican churches, many dating back to the 12th Century or earlier. The county has no city and no Anglican cathedral.

The county itself was originally part of the kingdom of Mercia, and is roughly divided in half by the river Severn. Consequently, Shropshire was occupied by two sub-kingdoms - Wreocensæte to the north of the river and Magonsæte to the south of the river. Shropshire was originally part of the diocese of Mercia, but that diocese became too large and so eventually the north of the county was put in the diocese of Lichfield, and the south in the diocese of Hereford.

The boundaries of the dioceses in Shropshire initially followed the boundary of the Magonsaete and Wreocensæte sub-kingdoms (part of Mercia). There have been various changes to the boundary over time, most notably in 1846 Bridgnorth deanery, on the abolition of the peculiar jurisdictions, was transferred to the diocese of Hereford (the deanery had previously been almost entirely exempt from the jurisdiction of the bishop of Lichfield). Then in 1905, the rural deanery of Condover was transferred to the diocese of Hereford, and some parishes were transferred from Hereford to Lichfield.

A small part of Shropshire was originally in a third diocese, St Asaph - Oswestry Deanery was transferred to the diocese of Lichfield from St Asaph in 1920 on the disestablishment of the Church in Wales.

St Oswald, Oswestry
